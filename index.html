The Mystery

The japanese "torii" has been a cultural and national highlight for Japan. The stillness and design of the gates have created a sense of mystery and serenity for tourists all over the world, as they fly in numbers to visit Japan and bask in the beauty and mystical nature of the country. However, what are torii gates? What do they symbolise or in other words... what do they mean?


These questions will all be answered in this website that is guided to extend your understanding of the unknown... let's head on in through the gates and explore!

What are Torii Gates?

The torii gates of Japan are a traditional gate which is heavily symbolic to the Japanese population. The gates pave the way towards accessing a new spiritual dimension as the gates act as the entry point towards many shrines in Japan. However, torii gates aren't always located as an entry point to shrines. The mysterious nature of the gates is brought about due to the various different locations they are identified in.


Some notable areas where torii gates are found in Japan are:

  • Lakes
  • Forests
  • Mountaintops
  • Ocean Coasts

These locations as serene as they are serve a symbolic purpose as to why torii gates have been created there. Torii gates that are found in nature like the locations listed above are said to embody deities and spiritual figures that are said to exist within that nature. Thus the purpose of the torii is to transcend the mortal plain and enter the spiritual dimension which hosts the kami (the god and deity of Japanese shintoism).


Although some people credit the origin of torii gates as having been derived from other parts of the world... many in Japan believe that the origin of torii gates is supported through a mythological text called Kojiki (古事記). In this story the goddess of the sun in Shintoism (Amaterasu Omikami) hid within a cave whilst another 8 million deities tried to grab her attention. In doing so they created a tree for the birds to chirp and rest, in this mythology this tree later became the first torii gate which is fascinating as (tori-iru) means bird perch giving a good amount of credibility to this mythology.

The Role of Shintoism

Shintoism is actively the biggest practiced religion/belief in all of Japan which is perhaps why torii gates are such an icon of the country. It is said that there are atleast 90,000 shinto shrines across all of Japan with every single one having atleast one torii gate marking it's entrance. The Japanese public therefore, hold the torii gates at a very high regard and respect. In addition, it is customary to bow before the gates paying homage to the "kami" that overlooks the torii.


Although some tourists enter torii gates right through their center which in shintoism, is travelling through a sacred portal to the dimension of the kami... Although many tend to walk along the edges of the gate to not distrub the gods (kami). It's in popular belief in shintoism that you must exit from the same torii gate that you walked into to access the mundane plain again.


Throughout the year, millions within Japan flock out to pay respect to shinto shrines across the country to celebrate special events such as the new year and cultural events such as Setsubun which is a Japanese festival honored to casting out evil spirits. This festival is relative to the appearance of torii gates in their colors across Japan, as many gates are coloured in vermillion due to belief that is casts away evil spirits and preserves the torii gate acting as protection for the human world.

The Structure of the Torii

There are believed to be around 60 different types of torii gates in Japan, all 60 types having variations from one another. These variations often come within their structure, color and sizes. There are two popular designs of the torii which are Shinmei and Myojin type torii's


Shinmei Torii Gates
  • Overall straight in it's design
  • Often made of natural wood
  • It has no incline (it is straight standing)

Myojin Torii Gates
  • Often found in vermillion colouring
  • Has a curved 'kasagi'(the top piece of wood)
  • Supported by a strut called 'Gakuzuka'
  • The name of the torii is often etched within these types of gates
